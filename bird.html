Conservation Feature

Keoladeo National Park, Bharatpur

From royal hunting reserve to UNESCO World Heritage Site, Keoladeo has evolved into one of Asia's most significant bird habitats, sheltering migratory and resident species across delicate wetland mosaics.

Conservation Timeline
  • Pre 1900s
    Royal Wetland Reserve

    The marshes were managed as a seasonal water body, attracting vast bird populations long before formal conservation labels existed.

  • 1971
    Sanctuary Declaration

    Keoladeo was officially designated as a bird sanctuary, shifting focus from hunting tradition to long-term ecological protection.

  • 1981
    National Park Status

    Legal status strengthened habitat safeguards and enabled more structured management of nesting zones and water cycles.

  • 1985
    UNESCO Recognition

    The park earned World Heritage status, acknowledging its global role in avian biodiversity and migratory bird conservation.

  • Today
    Wetland Under Pressure

    Conservation now focuses on water security, adaptive habitat restoration, and balancing tourism with fragile ecological rhythms.

Why Keoladeo Matters
Migration Hub

The park is a critical winter refuge for migratory birds traveling vast transcontinental routes, making each season globally significant.

Wetland Engineering

Its ecology depends on carefully managed water inflows; without seasonal water, feeding, breeding, and nesting cycles break down quickly.

Local Livelihoods

Eco-guides and community services connect conservation with livelihoods, creating local incentives to protect habitat quality year-round.

Biodiversity Indicator

Bird abundance here reflects broader environmental health, making Keoladeo a vital indicator for wetland resilience in north India.
