Bird Conservation at Keoladeo National Park

UNESCO World Heritage Rajasthan, India Wetland Ecosystem 350+ Bird Species

Keoladeo Ghana National Park in Bharatpur, Rajasthan is one of the world's most important bird sanctuaries. Originally a duck-hunting ground of the Maharajas of Bharatpur, it was declared a wildlife sanctuary in 1971 and a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985.

The park is a crucial wintering ground for the critically endangered Siberian Crane, which migrates over 6,400 km from western Siberia. Although the species has not been reliably sighted since 2002, conservation efforts continue to protect their potential habitat. Year-round residents include the Sarus Crane — the world's tallest flying bird at up to 1.8 m — along with painted storks, open-bill storks, spoonbills, darters, and over 350 other species.

The park's biggest threat is water scarcity. The Ajan Bund reservoir that feeds the wetlands receives diminishing monsoon runoff due to upstream diversions by farmers, causing the marshes to dry out earlier each year. Forest department managers now pump groundwater to maintain minimum water levels during nesting season. Community-based ecotourism, where local cycle-rickshaw guides earn livelihoods, has created strong local champions for the park's protection.
