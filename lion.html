Conservation Feature

Gir National Park & Wildlife Sanctuary

Nestled in Gujarat's semi-arid landscape, Gir is the sole refuge of the Asiatic Lion — a living testament to what focused conservation can achieve over decades.

Conservation Timeline
  • 1900s
    Edge of Extinction

    Overhunting reduced the Asiatic Lion population to just 20 individuals. The species stood on the brink of complete extinction on the subcontinent.

  • 1965
    Gir Declared a Sanctuary

    India formally declared Gir a wildlife sanctuary, providing legal protection to both the lions and their forest habitat.

  • 1968
    177 Lions Remain

    A census counted just 177 lions in the wild — a fragile population, but enough to signal that recovery was possible with sustained effort.

  • 2020
    Project Lion Launched

    India's government launched Project Lion to further protect Asiatic lions, improve habitat quality, and address human-wildlife conflict around Gir.

  • 2023
    674 Lions — A Triumph

    The most recent census recorded 674 Asiatic Lions — a near 30x recovery from the early 1900s and one of conservation's greatest success stories.

Why Gir Matters
Last Wild Population

Gir is the only place on Earth where Asiatic Lions exist in the wild, making its protection a global conservation priority.

Ecosystem Diversity

Beyond lions, Gir shelters leopards, hyenas, jackals, 300+ bird species, and the rare Indian star tortoise across scrub forest and grasslands.

Anti-Poaching

24/7 monitoring by frontline rangers and camera-trap networks have driven poaching incidents in Gir to near zero over the past decade.

Community

Maldhari pastoralists, who have lived alongside lions for centuries, are now active partners in conservation — their knowledge proving invaluable.
